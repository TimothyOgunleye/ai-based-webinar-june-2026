What to Expect:
"Encoding Values in AI Systems" explores how the cultural assumptions, priorities, and worldviews of a small number of dominant technology ecosystems are increasingly being embedded into the AI systems used across the world. As AI becomes more influential in communication, education, creativity, governance, and decision-making, the absence of diverse cultural representation in these systems creates significant social, ethical, and strategic concerns.
The talk examines how bias in AI extends beyond datasets and algorithms into deeper questions of language, identity, norms, values, and worldview representation. It highlights the risk of building globally deployed systems that unintentionally reflect narrow cultural perspectives while marginalizing others.
Designed for both technical and non-technical audiences, the session bridges AI engineering, policy, leadership, and culture. Technical professionals will gain insight into practical approaches for incorporating cultural context into AI design and evaluation, while business leaders, educators, policymakers, and creators will better understand the societal implications of culturally misaligned AI systems.
The session ultimately presents a strategic blueprint for developing more globally representative AI through intentional engineering, governance, localization, data stewardship, and inclusive participation. It reframes AI not merely as a technological system, but as a cultural infrastructure whose long-term impact will depend on whose values are encoded into it.

"Encoding Trust in LLM Systems" examines how trust can be systematically engineered into production-grade AI applications powered by Large Language Models (LLMs). Moving beyond high-level AI ethics discussions, the talk introduces a practical “Trust by Design” framework for developing secure, reliable, and governable AI systems.
The session presents an architectural approach to implementing layered safeguards across the LLM stack, including intent validation, retrieval grounding, hallucination mitigation, policy enforcement, observability, evaluation pipelines, and human-in-the-loop governance. It focuses on the engineering patterns and system-level decisions required to move foundation models safely from experimentation into enterprise deployment.
Targeted at AI, Data Science, and software engineering professionals, the talk provides actionable insights into designing resilient LLM-powered systems that balance capability with accountability. Attendees will gain a clearer understanding of how trust mechanisms can be embedded directly into AI infrastructure rather than treated as external controls.
As organizations increasingly integrate LLMs into critical workflows, the challenge is no longer just model performance, but operational trustworthiness. This talk highlights why the next generation of AI systems will be defined not only by intelligence, but by the robustness, transparency, and governance of the architectures behind them.
